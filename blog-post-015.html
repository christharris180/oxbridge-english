The Most Dangerous Words in the World: "Everyone Knows That"

There is an old saying — attributed to various wise figures throughout history, though the irony of nobody being quite sure who said it first is not lost — that if everyone is thinking alike, then nobody is really thinking at all.

Some of humanity's most catastrophic mistakes have not been born of ignorance. They have been born of something altogether more insidious: the quiet, almost invisible tyranny of conformity. The instinct to follow the crowd is ancient, wired deep into our psychology by thousands of years of evolution in which belonging to the group was, quite literally, the difference between survival and being eaten by something considerably larger than yourself. In the modern world, however — in boardrooms, parliaments, financial markets, laboratories and living rooms — that same instinct has an uncomfortable tendency to lead us, collectively and confidently, in entirely the wrong direction.

Two ideas illustrate this with particular elegance. One comes from the world of social psychology, the other from Israeli military intelligence. They emerged independently, separated by decades and disciplines, and yet they arrive at precisely the same conclusion: never mistake the majority view for the correct one simply because it happens to be the majority view.

The Asch Conformity Experiment

In the 1950s, the psychologist Solomon Asch conducted what would become one of the most quietly disturbing experiments in the history of social science.

Participants were shown a card displaying three lines of different lengths and asked a straightforward question: which line matched the reference line shown separately? The answer was not ambiguous. It was not a matter of interpretation. Under ordinary circumstances, virtually everyone got it right.

Then came the twist.

Each participant was seated alongside a group of individuals who were, unbeknownst to them, actors following a script. These actors would unanimously give the same incorrect answer before it was the real participant's turn to respond. And here is where it becomes genuinely unsettling: approximately 75% of participants conformed at least once, giving an answer they privately knew to be wrong because the room — that powerful, invisible force called the room — appeared to disagree with them.

What failed was not intelligence. The participants could see perfectly well which line was the right answer. What failed was something far more fundamental: independence. The desire to fit in, to avoid the discomfort of standing apart, proved strong enough to override the direct evidence of their own senses.

The Tenth Man Principle

The Tenth Man Principle has its origins in Israeli military intelligence, though its logic is so elegantly simple that one wonders why it took humanity quite so long to formalise it.

The premise works as follows. Ten intelligence analysts examine the same evidence and reach their conclusions. If nine of them arrive at identical judgments, the tenth is not merely permitted to disagree — they are required to. Not because they necessarily believe the consensus to be wrong. But because someone, as a matter of institutional duty, must actively and rigorously search for the evidence that suggests it might be.

The Tenth Man's role is to interrogate assumptions, pursue alternative explanations and test whether the other nine have — with the very best of intentions — collectively walked into the same cognitive trap. It is a role that demands a certain intellectual stubbornness, a willingness to be temporarily unpopular in the service of something more important than comfort: accuracy.

The principle gained broader cultural recognition through its portrayal in the film World War Z, though its underlying logic had been quietly at work in intelligence and risk management circles for considerably longer.

The Tenth Man is not the cynic, nor the contrarian, nor the tiresome colleague who disagrees on principle. He or she is, rather, the institutional insurance policy against the particular and dangerous phenomenon known as collective blindness.

Why These Two Ideas Belong Together

The Asch experiment explains, with rather uncomfortable precision, why people conform. The Tenth Man Principle explains, with admirable practicality, how to prevent it from becoming catastrophic.

One maps the psychological terrain. The other offers a navigational instrument for crossing it safely.

Together they illuminate something genuinely profound about how human beings make decisions in groups. The problem is almost never a lack of intelligence. The problem is a lack of disagreement — and the absence of anyone whose job it is to provide it. Consensus has a warmth to it, a reassuring solidity, that truth frequently and inconveniently lacks.

The Business World Rewards Independent Thinkers

There is considerable irony in the fact that the corporate world simultaneously celebrates teamwork and punishes the very disagreement that makes teams genuinely effective.

History has a long and unedifying record of companies that collapsed not because nobody noticed the warning signs, but because nobody felt sufficiently safe — or sufficiently empowered — to say so aloud. Employees observed problems. Engineers identified design flaws. Financial analysts quietly noted the risks accumulating on the balance sheet. And yet meetings proceeded, quarter after quarter, as elaborate exercises in confirming what everyone had already decided to believe rather than genuinely testing whether it deserved to be believed.

The organisations that have endured and thrived tend to be those that create deliberate, structured space for disagreement. Some formally appoint a devil's advocate during major decisions, someone whose explicit function is to challenge every assumption before the company commits itself irrevocably. It is not a comfortable role. It is, however, an extraordinarily valuable one.

Innovation has rarely emerged from rooms full of people nodding. It tends to arrive, often inconveniently, from the individual willing to ask the question nobody else wants to ask:

"What if we're wrong?"

The Same Trap in Our Personal Lives

It would be convenient to believe that conformity is primarily a corporate or institutional problem — something that happens to other people in other buildings. The evidence, unfortunately, suggests otherwise.

People choose careers because everyone agrees they lead to stability. They purchase property because everyone knows it only ever goes up in value. They invest in particular assets because the people around them are investing in them, and surely all those people cannot be mistaken. Sometimes these decisions work out perfectly well. Sometimes they produce the very bubbles — financial, social, ideological — that eventually and inevitably burst, taking a great deal of other people's confidence down with them.

History's most celebrated investors have tended to share one quality that has rather less to do with genius than is popularly supposed: the willingness to examine evidence independently of what the crowd happens to be thinking at that particular moment. Not because being different is inherently superior, but because independence of judgment is the only reliable defence against inheriting everyone else's mistakes.

How Cults Use Conformity

Nowhere, perhaps, is the mechanics of conformity more nakedly visible than in the phenomenon of cults — and nowhere is the popular understanding of them more consistently wrong.

The standard assumption is that cult members are somehow less intelligent, more credulous or more psychologically fragile than the rest of us. Research, with some persistence, keeps failing to support this. Cults rarely begin by asking their members to accept the extraordinary. They begin with the entirely ordinary: small agreements, modest commitments, shared language, a sense of belonging. One concession leads to another. Agreement becomes loyalty. Loyalty, in time, becomes identity. And once an idea is sufficiently entangled with who you are, disagreeing with it no longer feels like questioning a proposition — it feels like a form of self-betrayal.

The Asch experiment showed how powerful social pressure becomes even in something as trivially low-stakes as identifying the length of a line. Consider how exponentially stronger that pressure becomes when what depends upon your agreement is not merely the approval of strangers in a laboratory, but your friendships, your family, your reputation and your entire sense of who you are.

Religion and Shared Belief

The same psychological mechanisms operate, with varying intensity, within religious communities — though it is important to be precise about what this does and does not mean.

It does not mean that religious beliefs are therefore false. It means something altogether more modest and more interesting: that people often hold beliefs not only because they have examined and found them personally compelling, but also because those beliefs are shared, consistently reinforced and, crucially, rarely subjected to serious scrutiny within the community that holds them. The belief and the belonging become, over time, difficult to separate.

This observation is not peculiar to religion. It applies with equal force to political movements, investment groups, online communities, corporate cultures and the particular echo chambers that social media constructs around us with such cheerful efficiency. Wherever questioning becomes unwelcome, independent thinking quietly begins to atrophy.

"Everyone Knows That..."

Perhaps the most dangerous sequence of words in any language is the deceptively modest phrase: "Everyone knows that."

History has an extensive catalogue of things that everyone once knew with complete confidence. The Earth occupied the centre of the universe — this was not a fringe view, it was the educated consensus of centuries. Continents were fixed, permanent, immovable — the suggestion otherwise was considered faintly absurd until the evidence became impossible to ignore. Stomach ulcers were caused by stress and diet — it took decades of resistance before the role of bacteria was reluctantly accepted by a medical establishment that had, collectively, been looking elsewhere.

Financial markets have constructed entire bubbles on foundations of things that everyone knew to be obviously, permanently true.

Consensus is useful. It is not, however, proof. And there is a particular irony in the fact that the more universally an idea is accepted, the less likely anyone is to examine it — because the very universality of acceptance becomes, itself, the most convincing argument for it.

Unchallenged assumptions, regardless of their apparent popularity, deserve careful and periodic scrutiny. This is not cynicism. It is simply intellectual hygiene.

Becoming Your Own Tenth Man

None of this is an argument for reflexive contrarianism, which is merely conformity wearing a different costume and considerably more pleased with itself.

Independent thinking is not the automatic rejection of popular opinion. It is the disciplined habit of examining evidence before deciding what to believe about it — and remaining genuinely open to the possibility, however uncomfortable, that the answer might differ from the one the room has already settled upon.

A handful of questions, applied consistently, can do rather more for the quality of one's thinking than any number of clever frameworks:

  • What evidence actually supports this?
  • What evidence might contradict it?
  • What assumptions am I quietly making?
  • Who benefits if this belief becomes universal?
  • What would need to be true for the opposite to be correct?

These are not the questions of a troublemaker. They are the questions of a good manager, a sound investor, a wise leader — and, ultimately, a more honest thinker.

Final Thoughts

The Asch Conformity Experiment is a reminder — bracing, if one allows it to be — of how readily we abandon our own judgment the moment the room appears to disagree with us.

The Tenth Man Principle is a reminder that institutions, and individuals, require someone whose explicit function is to test whether the consensus has earned the confidence being placed in it.

Together they offer what is perhaps the most practically valuable lesson in critical thinking: the purpose of questioning is not to be difficult, to be clever, or to distinguish oneself through disagreement. The purpose is to get closer to the truth — which is, when one thinks about it, rather the whole point.

The next time someone informs you, with the quiet authority of the obviously correct, that everyone knows something to be true, resist the comfortable pull of agreement for just a moment.

Ask yourself one question — simple, ancient, and still, after all this time, genuinely subversive:

How do we know?
